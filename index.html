Introduction to 3D Printing

Brock M. Tice

Anfani LLC

What is 3D Printing?

  • Like a paper printer, but uses plastic
  • 1 layer at a time
  • The print head can move up (Z direction)
A 3D printer of the type most commonly used by hobbyists, a so-called 'Fused Deposition Modeling' printer, works by laying down one layer of plastic at a time, then moving the print head upward, and putting another layer on top. This method of 3D printing is simple and effective, but there are certain limits to what shapes can be successfully printed.

What is 3D Printing Useful For?

  • Design on the computer, then make it real
  • Download, modify, print, and share
  • Make objects that don't exist, can't be bought in stores
  • Prototype designs
3D printing allows you to design objects, whether artistic or functional, using powerful modeling tools on a computer, where you can make changes essentially for free until you are happy with the design. Then you can start the printer and make the object out of plastic. You can also easily share your designs with others, or download other people's designs from the Internet and then print them as they are, or make your own modifications, print, and share them. This enables more rapid development of useful designs, because everyone can build off of each others' work. Sometimes when designing a new tool or toy, or trying to fix something around the house, you need a piece of plastic in a shape that you cannot simply buy from the store, or even order from anywhere. That shape may not exist at all. In this case, you can design the shape you need and then print it. Later you can even use your 3D prints to create silicone or other types of molds and mass-produce copies yourself. Finally, if you are trying to design something, and need to test your design to see how well it fits or functions, 3D printing allows you to do this cheaply, before spending a lot of time and money on the tooling to have something manufactured.

Example: Blue Canary Night Light

  • Did not exist before
  • I knew how to sculpt shapes in Blender
  • Designed, went through a few prototypes
  • Finally finished piece and used to make molds, produce
  • Eventually went to mass-production via contract manufacturer
My first use of 3D printing was to develop a product, a blue canary night light. I designed the canary in a 3D modeling program called Blender, made some 3D renderings of it, and then sent off the design to a 3D printing service called Shapeways to have the first prototype printed. I tried mounting this on a night light I found at the store, decided it needed some tweaks and a change in size, and then had it printed again by Shapeways. Finally, I arrived at a design with which I was happy. I then sanded the print down, used it to make a silicone mold, and started producing my own copies using two-part urethane resin and blue dye. Eventually, I was able to prove to myself that the market existed for mass manufacturing, and hired a contract manufacturer to make the lights for me. I have been selling them for three years now, with hundreds of thousands of dollars in sales. I do not have the sculpting skills to have done this with hand tools, and the iterations of the design would have been much more costly in terms of time.

From Idea to Print (1 of 2)

  1. Have idea
  2. Model on computer
    • Sculpt - Blender, etc
    • CAD - OpenSCAD, Freecad, Autodesk, etc
  3. Slice - convert to printer instructions
    • Skeinforge - old way, more sophisticted, slow
    • Slic3er - new way, faster, smarter, easier
The process of going from a an idea to a 3D print involves several steps. First, you have to translate the idea into a computer model of some sort. Typically you use an art-type program (Blender or various commercial packages, Google SketchUp) to sculpt things, a computer-aided-drafting or CAD program (OpenSCAD, Freecad, stuff by Autodesk) for things that need to have specific dimensions, or you can even take data from the real world (3D scanner, medical imaging) and convert that into a computer model. There are limits to what a fused deposition modeling printer can print. Large overhangs will either fail (can't print into empty space), or require you to add support material to the design or the processing. Once the computer model is created, it must be processed into instructions for the printer. This is done using a so-called slicing program, because it slices the model into layers. This step will have problems if your model is not water-tight, or has other problems like incorrect normals. There are many guides online for fixing these problems. This step is also very sensitive to the settings you use for slicing. They must be correct for your printer, for the plastic you're using, and even for the physical environment around the printer (temperature, air movement). Generally you will want to review your settings each time you make a print to be sure that they match your goal. For instance, for mechanical objects you usually want a solid print. For artistic objects, a hollow or mostly-hollow print may suffice and will make your print quicker, cheaper, and lighter.

From Idea to Print (2/2)

  1. Load into printer control software (Pronterface, repsnapper, repraphost, ReplicatorG)
  2. Prepare printer (warm bed, hot end)
  3. Print
  4. Remove from bed/cleanup
The slicing program outputs instructions for the printer. These are loaded into what's known as 'host software', a program used to control the printer before, during, and after a print. The host software is used to warm up any relevant parts of the printer, position the component axes of the printer appropriately, and start, run, and finish the print. Finally, once the print is complete, it must be carefully pried from the print bed (if using kapton or blue painters' tape), or it will separate from a glass bed once allowed to cool. You may find strings, blobs, or other defects in the print that are usually cleaned up with a sharp hobby knife or a pocketknife. Prints may then be finished if desired by sanding, painting, or acetone bath (beyond the scope of this presentation).

Parts of a RepRap 3D Printer (1/2)

  • Frame
    • Main Frame
    • X Axis
    • Y Axis
    • Z Axis
  • Electronics and Wiring (controller, steppers, endstops)
  • Heated Bed
On RepRap type printers, the X axis generally moves the print head (extruder) side-to-side, the Y axis generally moves the print via the bed, and the Z axis generally moves the print head up from layer to layer. The electronics translate the printer instructions from the slicing software into movement of the printer, changes in temperature, and so on. The heated bed helps plastic to adhere during the print, and cools down to release the printed item when finished.

Parts of a RepRap 3D Printer (2/2)

  • Extruder Assembly
    • Extruder Motor
    • Drive Components (gear, idler)
    • Hot End
      • Barrel
      • Insulator
      • Heating Element
      • Thermistor
      • Nozzle
The extruder assembly takes in room-temperature plastic at one and, and pushes soft, hot thermoplastic of a different diameter out the other end. The motor drives a gear of some type (sprocket, hobbed bolt), which physically squeezes the filament and digs into it (possibly against an idler of some kind), pushing the filament into the hot end. The hot end consists of a barrel, held in an insulator to keep it from melting the printer and retain heat for heating plastic, a heating element to warm the barrel and the plastic, a thermistor to monitor the temperature, and a nozzle to shape the hot thermoplastic as it exits the barrel. If the nozzle gets too cold, the printing will stop, and the plastic will jam or strip out in the extruder assembly.

Print Materials (1/2)

Thermoplastics (conventional), exotic things like frosting, pen for plotting and circuit board etching, etc. The two main materials used today are PLA and ABS, and they are comparable in cost.
  • PLA
    • Poly-Lactic Acid
    • Very safe, technically edible
    • Smells nice when printing, like hot maple syrup
    • Can be made from biological sources
    • Prints at a lower temperature than ABS (~180C)
    • Requires lower bed temperature than ABS (~60C)
    • More rigid than ABS, but more brittle too
    • Less prone to warping than ABS
    • Typically available in 1.75mm or 3mm diameters
PLA is easy to print with but has some drawbacks relating to material strength and durability.

Print Materials (2/2)

  • ABS
    • Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene
    • It's what Legos are made of
    • Smells like burning plastic when printing, can harm birds
    • Generally made from petroleum sources
    • Prints at limits of printer hardware (220-240C)
    • Requires bed heat at limits of printer hardware (110-130C)
    • More flexible than PLA, more impact-resistant
    • More prone to warping on large prints than PLA
    • Typically available in 1.75mm or 3mm diameters
ABS is more tricky to print with than PLA, but has desirable advantages over PLA in the areas of strength and durability.

Safety

  • Electricity Watch out for mains power at power supply connectors
  • Heat The hot end gets up to more than 2x the boiling point of water, will burn you instantly. Print bed can exceed boiling point of water.
  • Pinch Points The bed, X carriage, whole X axis, and belts and pulleys can all snag skin, clothing, and hair. Keep these things clear.
Think before you touch.
